What Happens Once Every 75 Years?
Leah Prentice

Last year, the Esquimalt Graving Dock celebrated its 75th anniversary. On Canada Day, the Dock held its first-ever Open House and dozens of PWGSC employees and their families volunteered to ensure everyone had an old fashioned good time.

The highlight of the event was a re-enactment of the original opening day, July 1, 1927. A parade of vintage vehicles drove dignitaries down the hill for the rededication ceremony.

Jack Gale, a crane operator at the Dock was on hand to help Environment Minister David Anderson and Esquimalt Mayor Ray Rice dedicate a time capsule.

People came to the Open House to learn about the Dock's history and how it is positioning itself for the 21st century. Rocky Domingo, a Senior Project Manager with Professional and Technical Services, led dock tours.

Doug Ferrier, an Implementation Officer with Professional and Technical services shows-off the Pumphouse - a heritage building with original one-of-a-kind 1927 machinery.

Many former employees came back for a visit.

PWGSC volunteers helped with carnival games, face painting and a hotdog barbeque by donation that helped raise over $1,300 for the United Way.

Bob Graves of Professional and Technical Services (far left, red shirt), Kelly Graves - Bob's daughter (red shirt) and Austin Cabral, Regional Director of Finance and Administration (red suspenders) volunteered their time at the carnival games.

What do you get when you task six teams with building a "ship" without using nails, screws or power tools and capable of crossing the finish line first? Shipyard Wars.

And They're Off!

Peggy Stewart (shorts) and Jim Milne (in suit wearing hat) of the Esquimalt Graving Dock give out prizes.

Hats off to all our volunteers. Without them this day would not have been possible. Thank you!
